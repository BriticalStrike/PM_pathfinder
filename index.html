Agile, Scrum, & the Library

A research guide for agile project management and Scrum within the context of LIS.

Introduction

As libraries are oftentimes becoming more and more unpredictable in their programming, tending to bend to the needs of its community, it is getting more important for librarians to think on their toes. Librarians can be juggling many different projects at a time while working with a myriad of people including staff, stakeholders, community leaders, and patrons. Libraries are also oftentimes iterating over their programs and trying to come up with the best possible solutions to community problems. This makes libraries great candidates for utilizing agile project management, and even in some cases, Scrum. The ability to adapt agile principles and strategies to any given project and work with a team-based approach is essential. This pathfinder will help library staff research agile project management and Scrum to apply essential principles and theory to their own libraries. In addition to essential tools and resources for agile and Scrum, this pathfinder includes articles that frame libraries in a startup-type context and relate the diverse and innovative projects to software development, the main contributor to agile project management literature.

The scope of this pathfinder is a close look at agile project management and Scrum, with additional research to frame these resources within the context of library and information science (LIS). This pathfinder does not take a look at library management theory in general and has been created for an audience that is alrady knowledgeable about LIS.

Websites

Gateway Sites

  • The Agile Alliance: The Agile Alliance is a nonprofit organization dedicated to agile software development. The website gives access to important agile resources, including foundational resources and principles of agile theory, an agile glossary, and a practices timeline and map. The site also has community resources, blogs, and lists upcoming events. While this is related to software development, the resources it contains are applicable to other industry projects and are a good place to start to situate oneself in the world of agile project management.

  • APM's Agile Project Management Page: This webpage put together by the Association for Project Management, Europe's largest professional organization for project management, contains the definition of agile project management, benefits, and videos. The website also has agile-related resources including a glossary, blogs, and articles about agile project management. In addition to these free resources, the site has recommendations for books on agile. These sources can be used to gain a significant base understanding of agile project management before being applied to the smaller scope of library management.

  • Scrum.org: A main portal and guide site that contains resources and information about Scrum, an agile project management framework. It was created by one of the founders of the Scrum framework, Ken Schwaber, and contains the Scrum guide free to review or download, as well as community forums, videos, training, guides, and blogs. This is a primary source for Scrum-related information.

Specific Websites

  • ProjectManagement.com: This website was created by project managers for project managers. It is a conglomeration of project management tools and resources. There are links to webinars, a community site with blogs, and forums where users can ask questions or talk with others in project management. It also has a tools category with training, podcasts, software resources, and links to project management-related events.

  • DZone: A community of technology professionals who create content for the website. The site’s editorial board reviews submitted content to post. The site contains an entire category dedicated to agile project management and agile web. It has news articles, books, and resources on Scrum. While it is not wholly dedicated to project management, there are a lot of diamonds on this site for agile project managers and it is a good place to get an overview of agile project management in an innovative and fast-paced environment, such as software development.

Blogs

Blogs are a great resource for keeping your finger on the pulse of a topic. This is a list of blogs related to agile project management and Scrum.

  • Mountain Goat Software: Mike Cohn, the author of this blog, is one of the co-founders of the Scrum framework and works with software development teams during their project management phases. He is a consultant on agile project management and Scrum. Many of his blog posts are about Scrum and agile project management and cover a variety of topics related to these topics, such as: management styles, tips and tricks for project management, and some more fun agile and Scrum-related posts.

  • Scrum.org's Blog: Scrum.org has its own community and blog. The blog posts are all things agile and Scrum-related. There are a variety of authors and each post is tagged, allowing you to find similar posts easily.

Open-Access Journals

Project Management-Related Journals

There are quite a few project management journals out there, however, they are almost all subscription-based. If you want to find journal articles on agile project management, it is best to search through databases accessible through a school, library, or use Google Scholar in conjunction with the keyword searches provided here. There is, however, one project management-based journal that allows access to its articles.

  • The European Project Management Journal: An academic journal that publishes research on current project management trends and issues. While this does not specifically relate to agile project management, there are articles that are relevant to agile and Scrum.

LIS Journals

The following are open-access journals for information science. While these journals may not have many articles on project management-related topics, they are nonetheless great resources to search and great for providing context to any LIS-related research.

  • The International Federation of Library Associations and Institutions (IFLA) Journal: A peer-reviewed academic journal for library and information science. The IFLA is a global association recognized for its dedication to LIS. This journal is open-access and contains articles on many different LIS topics. While many articles would not be on project management specifically, there are articles that could relate to project management or to the future projects libraries may need to take on.

  • Information Research: An international peer-reviewed open-access journal for all information-related topics. This is a very broad journal and would be a great place to try and find articles related to libraries and project management or agile project management in information professions.

Articles

Research Articles

  • Forsman, D. & Hansson, P. (2014). “Introducing agile principles and management to a library organization.” Proceedings of the IATUL Conferences. Paper 1.

    Daniel Forsman is the library director at Chalmers University in Sweden and Peter Hansson is the Head of Administration in Communication and Learning in Science at Chalmers University. This conference paper delineates the use of agile project management and Scrum at Chalmers University to keep up with demanding changes in technology, user needs, and budgeting issues.

  • Wertman, L., Campbell, C. & Rinna, G. (2019, January). “Optimizing library services- Agile management of electronic resources: A Charleston Conference presentation.” Against the Grain 29 (6). doi 10.7771/2380-176X.7900

    The author, Geraldine Rinna, is the Electronic Resources Librarians at Western Michigan University. The column editors are Lindsay Wertman, Managing Director at IGI Global, and Caroline Campbell, Promotions Assistant at IGI Global. At the University of Michigan, the library team adopted agile project management tools and practices to keep up with a change in their library services platform. This article summarizes the agile strategies the University of Michigan Library adopted to gain control of their major project.

Context Articles

  • Mathews, B. (2012, April). Think like a startup [White paper].

    Brian Mathews is the Associate Dean for Learning and Outreach at Virginia Tech. This white paper takes the culture of startups and applies this to library programming. It is meant to shake up the idea of a library and apply some of the more innovative practices startups use. As agile project management and, more pointedly, Scrum is oftentimes used in software startups, this type of thinking ties contemporary library thought into the more innovative and active project management styles.

  • Mathews, B., Metko, S. & Tomlin, P. (2018, May 7). Empowerment, experimentation, engagement: Embracing partnership models in libraries. EDUCAUSE Review.

    EDUCAUSE is a nonprofit organization dedicated to bringing knowledge of information technology to higher education. Mathews coauthors this paper with Stefanie Metko, Director of Teaching & Learning Engagement at Virginia Tech, and Patrick Tomlin, the Director of Learning Environments at Virginia Tech. This article reviews the transformation of libraries from transactional book archives to partnered community centerpieces for universities. It delineates different project university libraries have undertaken to meet the needs of their patrons. It is a context piece for the significance of the research of agile project management in libraries. This article shows a shift in library programming and the need to facilitate project management strategies that are adapted to the modern library.

  • Mattern, S. (2014, June).Library as infrastructure. Places Journal.

    Places Journal is a was founded at MIT and Berkeley in 1983 and continues to produce articles on trends in architecture and landscape. This article outlines the future of libraries as infrastructure and is a context piece for the importance of research on libraries using agile project management to manage their programs and services. It outlines different projects libraries have taken on to meet the growing needs of their communities..

Databases

Open-Access Databases

The following databases are free to use without a subscription. Used in conjunction with the subject headings and keywords found here, a significant amount of articles can be found for this pathfinder's topic.

  • The Directory of Open Access Journals: A directory of open-access peer-reviewed journals and articles. It contains over 13,700 journals from 130 countries. There is an advanced search function and the ability to filter results by article or journal.

  • Google Scholar: A Google-run engine for academic resources. It has the ability to search for open-access or .pdfs of articles related to the topic. It pulls from all parts of the Internet, not just one database, so it will sometimes find resources that are restricted. Oftentimes, however, it will find articles that may be restricted on one site, but have a .pdf available on another site.

Subscription-Based Databases

The following are subscription-based databases. While some of them grant access to open-access articles or journals, many of the articles in them are restricted. If something relevant is found, it can be searched for on one of the above databases or found through a local or school library's subscription. These databases are ranked in order of their usefulness without a subscription.

  • Emerald Insight: An academic database with access to journals, articles, and books. Search queries can be filtered to only include articles that are open-access or available with the school or local library subscription.

  • Taylor & Francis Online: A subscription-based database for searching journals, articles, and books. It contains a myriad of topics and can be searched without a subscription. It also provides access to open-access journals here where you might find an article you can read online.

  • EBSCOHost: A collection of research databases that could prove very useful for finding research articles on agile project management and information professions. It is a well-known leading database company in academia, but access to articles is much more restricted than the previous databases.

Keywords, Phrases, and Subject Headings

When searching for library-related articles, it is important to combine subject keywords such as “agile AND project management AND libraries” or “scrum AND libraries”. While searching through these articles, I found it interesting that many of them also contained keywords like the list found below under "Tags found on relevant articles". Depending on what type of article you need for a specific type of paper, whether it be about managing collections, teams, or digital services, it would be important to include those keywords in a search, as well, which might look something like “agile AND libraries AND digital services”.

My keyword searches:

  • agile
  • agile practices
  • agile project management
  • libraries
  • project management
  • scrum

Tags found on relevant articles:

  • agile software development
  • collection development
  • organizational change
  • strategic planning
  • teamwork
  • workflow
  • workflow analysis

Research Advice

Start the groundwork for understanding agile project management and Scrum via the websites provided. Refer to these websites for glossary terms or trends in the world of agile project management. Once you have a good idea of what you are looking for, utilize your local library and school library’s subscription databases and search for the keywords provided. In this case, EBSCOHost, TandFOnline, and EmeraldInsight were the most helpful, but all were accessed through San Jose State University’s King Library Databases. If some articles seem relevant, but have restricted access, try finding them on Google Scholar or search the doi with “pdf” in the search bar. Sometimes, articles will be restricted on one site, but there will be another website that has a .pdf version of the article.

Remember, research is iterative and oftentimes you may find yourself revisiting articles, websites, and community forums. Don’t be afraid to search community questions and answers to find what you are looking for or to even find what you you should be looking for. This pathfinder serves as a base of understanding and a starting point to researching agile project management, Scrum, and how it can be applied to libraries.

Reflection

I believe this list is extensive enough to gather all research on the topic at hand. The resources provided give a great overview of agile project management and Scrum, while the LIS-related articles and journals provide a foundation for beginning to think of the library in terms of fast-paced futuristic project hubs that will need to adopt agile project management strategies to keep up with growing community needs.
